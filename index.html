How I Bought My First Income-Producing Property Nearly a Decade Ago Through a FSBO Transaction

Nearly a decade ago, I bought my first income-producing property, and it happened to be a For Sale By Owner (FSBO) deal. What made it unique wasn't just the property itself, but the fact that I represented myself as the buyer throughout the entire process.

From making the initial offer to managing due diligence and coordinating closing, I handled the transaction directly. It turned out to be a smooth and successful purchase that ultimately became a long-term, income-generating investment for nearly ten years.

Making the Offer Directly to the Seller

Since the property was FSBO, I worked directly with the seller to structure and submit the offer without a buyer's agent involved.

That meant clear, direct communication from the beginning and making sure both parties fully understood the terms without any intermediary interpretation.

Managing Due Diligence Independently

Once the offer was accepted, I was responsible for managing the entire due diligence process, including:

  • Scheduling and reviewing inspections
  • Evaluating disclosures and property condition
  • Tracking contractual deadlines
  • Coordinating financing and appraisal milestones

Even without representation, the process itself remained structured. The difference was that I had to be highly organized and proactive to ensure nothing was missed.

Letting the Title Company Handle Closing

The title company played a key role in handling the legal and administrative side of the transaction, including:

  • Title search and clearance
  • Escrow coordination
  • Final settlement statements
  • Recording the deed

Their involvement ensured the transaction was completed properly and in compliance with all legal requirements.

Negotiation Dynamics and Self-Representation

In this type of transaction, representing myself as the buyer introduced a different dynamic.

One potential advantage is negotiation flexibility. Without a buyer's agent involved, there can be more direct communication and a simpler structure to the transaction for both parties.

In some cases, it can also create an efficiency advantage for the seller, since the traditional buyer's agent commission does not factor into the deal structure in the same way. Depending on how an offer is structured, that flexibility can sometimes influence pricing or terms.

From my perspective, the biggest benefit wasn't simply cost savings, it was having direct control over communication, timing, and decision-making throughout the process.

That said, this approach does come with added responsibility. There is no buffer for missed details, miscommunication, or process errors.

The Outcome

The transaction closed successfully without complications, and the property went on to become a consistent income-producing asset for almost a decade.

Looking back, the value wasn't just in the investment itself, but in gaining a full understanding of how a real estate transaction works from start to finish.

Pros and Cons of a FSBO Self-Represented Purchase (Buyer Side)

Pros

  • Direct communication with the seller
  • Greater control over timing and decision-making
  • Potential for more flexible negotiation structure
  • No reliance on third-party interpretation of terms
  • Strong learning experience and process transparency

Cons

  • Full responsibility for managing deadlines and paperwork
  • No professional buffer for negotiation strategy
  • Higher risk of missing important details if not organized
  • Requires comfort with contracts, inspections, and transaction flow
  • Can be time-intensive and detail-heavy throughout the process

Final Thoughts

FSBO transactions can be highly effective in the right situation, but they are not passive experiences. They require organization, attention to detail, and a willingness to take full responsibility for the process.

In my case, it worked out well and became a foundational investment experience that lasted nearly a decade. But like most things in real estate, success comes down to preparation, awareness, and execution, not just opportunity.

HOA vs. Community Covenants: They're Not the Same Thing

One of the biggest misconceptions I encounter during real estate due-diligence is the assumption that no HOA means no restrictions. Unfortunately, that's not always the case.

Many buyers (and even experienced real estate professionals) are surprised to learn that a subdivision can have recorded community covenants that remain legally enforceable even when there is no active Homeowners Association (HOA).

What Is an HOA?

A Homeowners Association is an organization responsible for managing and enforcing the rules of a community. HOAs often collect dues, maintain common areas, and oversee architectural approvals and other community standards.

When purchasing property in an HOA, buyers typically know they should expect governing documents, assessments, and ongoing obligations.

What Are Community Covenants?

Community covenants, also known as Covenants, Conditions, and Restrictions (CC&Rs), are recorded legal documents that "run with the land." They are attached to the property itself and continue to apply regardless of ownership changes. These covenants can exist with or without an active HOA. Depending on the subdivision, recorded covenants may include restrictions on:

  • Building size and setbacks
  • Exterior materials or architectural standards
  • RV, boat, or trailer parking
  • Livestock or certain animals
  • Home-based businesses
  • Fencing requirements
  • Short-term rentals
  • Further subdivision of the property

In some cases, neighboring property owners, or other parties identified in the covenants, may still have the legal authority to enforce these restrictions, even if there is no HOA collecting dues or holding meetings.

Why This Matters During Due Diligence

A title commitment will often list recorded declarations and restrictive covenants as exceptions to title insurance. However, these documents may not receive the attention they deserve during the transaction.

It's easy to assume that "no HOA" means complete freedom to use the property as you wish. That assumption can lead to unpleasant surprises after closing.

Whether you're planning to build a shop, park an RV, raise animals, operate a business from home, or simply make improvements to your property, existing covenants may affect your plans.

The Bottom Line

Don't confuse the absence of an HOA with the absence of restrictions.

Part of thorough due-diligence is reviewing the recorded documents that affect a property, not just determining whether an HOA exists. Understanding these restrictions before closing can help buyers make informed decisions and avoid costly surprises later.

If you're purchasing property, taking the time to review recorded covenants is one of the most valuable steps you can take before signing on the dotted line.

Not All Realtors Are Created Equal: How to Spot a Great One (and Avoid a Bad One)

Like any profession, real estate has a wide range of skill levels, ethics, and work habits. There are outstanding realtors who guide clients smoothly through one of the biggest financial decisions of their lives, and there are others who fall short, sometimes significantly.

The good news is that most problems can be avoided if you know what to look for upfront. Whether you're buying or selling, here are the key traits that separate a great realtor from a poor one.

1. Strong Communication Skills

A good realtor communicates clearly, consistently, and proactively. You should expect:

  • Quick responses to calls, texts, and emails
  • Honest updates, even when the news isn't ideal
  • Clear explanations of contracts, timelines, and next steps

🚩 Constantly chasing them for updates or clarity is a big red flag.

2. Deep Local Market Knowledge

Great realtors understand their local market beyond surface-level listings. They know:

  • Neighborhood trends and pricing patterns
  • School districts, amenities, and development plans
  • What homes actually sell for, not just listing prices

✨ This knowledge helps buyers avoid overpaying and helps sellers price strategically from day one.

3. Honest Guidance (Not Just What You Want to Hear)

A strong realtor isn't there to "yes" everything, they're there to guide you correctly. That means they will:

  • Tell a seller if their price expectation is unrealistic
  • Advise a buyer when a home is overpriced or not a good fit
  • Provide data-driven recommendations, not emotional guesses

🚩 A realtor who agrees with everything just to win or keep your business is a big red flag.

4. Proven Experience and Track Record

Experience matters. But it's not just about years in the business. Look for:

  • A consistent history of closed transactions
  • Experience in your specific type of transaction (first-time buyers, investment properties, luxury homes, etc.)
  • Positive client testimonials or referrals

5. Strong Negotiation Skills

One of the biggest values a realtor brings is negotiation. A good realtor:

  • Knows how to structure competitive offers
  • Advocates firmly but professionally on your behalf
  • Understands when to push and when to pivot

🚨 Bad negotiation can cost buyers thousands or cost sellers serious profit!

6. Attention to Detail

Real estate transactions involve contracts, deadlines, inspections, disclosures, and legal obligations. A great realtor:

  • Double-checks paperwork
  • Tracks critical deadlines
  • Ensures nothing falls through the cracks

🚩 Missed deadlines, sloppy paperwork, or confusion during closing is a big red flag.

7. Professional Network

Top realtors don't work alone, they have trusted connections, including:

  • Mortgage lenders
  • Inspectors
  • Contractors
  • Title companies

✨ This network helps make the process smoother and can save clients time, money, and stress.

8. Integrity Over Commission

This is the most important trait of all.

A good realtor prioritizes long-term reputation over short-term gain. They don't:

  • Pressure clients into bad deals
  • Hide information
  • Rush you into decisions for the sake of a quick commission

Instead, they aim to build trust and earn repeat business and referrals.

Final Thoughts

Real estate doesn't have "one-size-fits-all" professionals. The difference between a good experience and a stressful one often comes down to choosing the right agent.

Take your time, ask questions, and trust your instincts. A great realtor will make you feel informed, supported, and confident, not rushed or confused.

Why Staging Matters More Than Most People Think

I don't have a formal certificate in staging, but what I do have is a critical eye for design, presentation, and how people emotionally respond to a space.

Good staging isn't about decorating. It's about positioning a property so buyers can instantly understand its potential.

First Impressions Happen Fast

Most buyers decide how they feel about a home within the first few seconds of walking in, or even earlier from photos online.

That means staging isn't optional in today's market, it's strategic.

A well-staged home:

  • Feels brighter and more open
  • Helps buyers visualize themselves living there
  • Reduces distractions from the actual structure and layout
  • Photographs significantly better for listings

A poorly presented home does the opposite; it makes even great properties feel smaller, darker, or less valuable than they really are.

Staging Is About Space, Not Stuff

One of the biggest misconceptions about staging is that it's about adding more décor. In reality, it's often the opposite.

The best staging usually involves:

  • Removing unnecessary furniture
  • Simplifying layouts to improve flow
  • Creating visual breathing room
  • Neutralizing overly personal design choices

The goal is not to impress, it's to depersonalize and clarify.

Why a "Critical Eye" Matters

You don't need a staging certificate to notice when a room feels "off". A strong staging mindset is really about asking simple questions:

  • Does this room feel open or cramped?
  • Is the furniture scale appropriate?
  • What is the buyer's eye drawn to first?
  • Does anything distract from the home's best features?

Small adjustments, furniture placement, lighting, spacing, can completely change how a space feels.

The Hidden ROI of Staging

Well-staged homes tend to:

  • Sell faster
  • Attract stronger first impressions online
  • Generate more showings
  • Reduce price reductions later

In many cases, staging doesn't just "help", it directly impacts net proceeds.

It's one of the few areas in real estate where relatively small upfront effort can significantly influence final outcome.

Final Thought

Staging isn't about perfection or interior design awards, it's about presentation psychology.

You don't need formal credentials to recognize when a space is working or when it's not. You just need to understand how buyers see, feel, and interpret what they're walking into.

And in real estate, that perception often becomes reality.

A Neighbor with a Passion for Conservation

Most people know Paul Tudor Jones as a legendary investor, but I've always thought of him through a more local, personal lens.

My family owns a parcel of land that once included wetlands, rendering part of it unsuitable for development. Rather than leaving those unbuildable acres idle, we sold them to our neighbor, none other than Paul Tudor Jones. That decision, weighed carefully at the time, ultimately aligned with something larger: keeping land like that in the hands of someone who values open space.

Over time, I was curious to learn more about Tudor Jones's broader conservation work.

Beyond finance, Tudor Jones has supported major environmental efforts, including the restoration of the Everglades through The Everglades Foundation, as well as land and habitat preservation projects in places like Colorado and East Africa.

What stands out to me is the idea that wealth and influence can be directed toward protecting landscapes that might otherwise be lost to development. In the case of my family's transaction, it is the very wetlands that I look out onto from my family's wrap-around porch that remain undeveloped and part of a larger natural ecosystem.

It's a reminder that sometimes the most lasting investments aren't financial at all, they're the places we choose to preserve.

Why I Chose to Manage My Own Rental Property

When we bought our rental property, I made a deliberate decision to manage it myself rather than hire a property manager. I wanted full control of our investment, and a real understanding of what it takes to run a business.

I also chose to manage it because I knew I could. I had the experience and know-how to get things done effectively, and I trusted my ability to stay on top of the details and make sound decisions.

I knew there would be a learning curve, and I welcomed the challenge. If I was going to invest in real estate, I wanted to understand the business in depth, even if that meant learning through trial by fire.

What I quickly discovered is that owning a rental requires systems, communication, consistency, and sound judgment. Even with careful screening and clear leases in place, you still encounter situations where real life doesn't fit neatly within the terms of a contract. As a landlord, you're constantly working to balance protecting your investment with responding appropriately to human circumstances.

One of the most important parts of the experience has been doing it as a team with my husband. I come from a legal background, so my instinct is to be structured, detail-oriented, and firm about enforcing rules. My husband naturally takes a more relaxed, relationship-focused approach. At times, I would lean toward strict enforcement, while he encouraged stepping back to consider context and the human side of a situation. Other times, my structure helped keep us consistent and aligned. Reflecting back on it now, I'm convinced that it's this very balance that led to our success.

Overall, I wouldn't trade the experience. It has made me a more informed investor and given me a deeper understanding of what it really means to be responsible for an investment, and for others.

Sometimes the Best Real Estate Deal Is the One You Walk Away From

Over the years, I've walked away from more deals than I can count. Some of those properties checked every box. I could already picture the renovations and finished product.

But then came the title investigation.

Uncovering the Legal Story

Before I commit to purchasing any property, I spend time digging into its history. This is one of my absolute favorite things to do. I'm not just looking at the home itself, I'm looking at the property's legal story. Who owned it? Are there unresolved liens? Easements? Boundary disputes? Probate issues? Unreleased mortgages? Restrictive covenants? Tax problems? Access concerns?

These are the kinds of issues that can turn what looks like a great investment into an expensive mistake.

Beyond the Realtor's Scope

While real estate agents do an incredible job helping buyers and sellers navigate the transaction, a detailed title investigation typically falls outside the scope of what a Realtor is hired to do. Their expertise is marketing, negotiations, contracts, and guiding clients through the buying and selling process. The deeper legal research into a property's title is a separate layer of due diligence that every serious buyer should consider before closing.

I've had properties that looked perfect on the surface, only to uncover issues that would have cost thousands of dollars, or years of legal headaches, to resolve. As disappointing as it was to let those opportunities go, I've never regretted walking away from a bad deal.

Emotion vs. Discipline

In real estate, emotions can be powerful. It's easy to fall in love with a property. But successful investing requires balancing excitement with careful research. Sometimes the smartest investment decision isn't saying "yes", it's having the discipline to say "no."

Every property has a story. My job is to uncover that story before I become part of it.

Due Diligence as Investment

That's why I believe due diligence isn't just another step in the buying process, it's one of the most important investments you can make. A thorough title investigation can reveal risks that aren't visible during a showing or even during a standard inspection.

At the end of the day, I'd rather lose a deal than inherit someone else's problems. And if that means walking away from a property I truly wanted, I'll do it every time.

Protecting Your Investment

Because protecting your investment always comes before closing the deal.
