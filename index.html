HOA vs. Community Covenants: They're Not the Same Thing

One of the biggest misconceptions I encounter during real estate due-diligence is the assumption that no HOA means no restrictions. Unfortunately, that's not always the case.

Many buyers (and even experienced real estate professionals) are surprised to learn that a subdivision can have recorded community covenants that remain legally enforceable even when there is no active Homeowners Association (HOA).

What Is an HOA?

A Homeowners Association is an organization responsible for managing and enforcing the rules of a community. HOAs often collect dues, maintain common areas, and oversee architectural approvals and other community standards.

When purchasing property in an HOA, buyers typically know they should expect governing documents, assessments, and ongoing obligations.

What Are Community Covenants?

Community covenants, also known as Covenants, Conditions, and Restrictions (CC&Rs), are recorded legal documents that "run with the land." They are attached to the property itself and continue to apply regardless of ownership changes. These covenants can exist with or without an active HOA. Depending on the subdivision, recorded covenants may include restrictions on:

  • Building size and setbacks
  • Exterior materials or architectural standards
  • RV, boat, or trailer parking
  • Livestock or certain animals
  • Home-based businesses
  • Fencing requirements
  • Short-term rentals
  • Further subdivision of the property

In some cases, neighboring property owners, or other parties identified in the covenants, may still have the legal authority to enforce these restrictions, even if there is no HOA collecting dues or holding meetings.

Why This Matters During Due Diligence

A title commitment will often list recorded declarations and restrictive covenants as exceptions to title insurance. However, these documents may not receive the attention they deserve during the transaction.

It's easy to assume that "no HOA" means complete freedom to use the property as you wish. That assumption can lead to unpleasant surprises after closing.

Whether you're planning to build a shop, park an RV, raise animals, operate a business from home, or simply make improvements to your property, existing covenants may affect your plans.

The Bottom Line

Don't confuse the absence of an HOA with the absence of restrictions.

Part of thorough due-diligence is reviewing the recorded documents that affect a property, not just determining whether an HOA exists. Understanding these restrictions before closing can help buyers make informed decisions and avoid costly surprises later.

If you're purchasing property, taking the time to review recorded covenants is one of the most valuable steps you can take before signing on the dotted line.
